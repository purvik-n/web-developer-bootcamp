The chicken (Gallus gallus domesticus) is a domesticated form of the red junglefowl (Gallus gallus), originally native to Southeast Asia. It was first[1] domesticated around 8,000 years ago and is one of the most common and widespread domesticated animals in the world. Chickens are primarily kept for their meat and eggs, though they are also kept as pets.

As of 2023, the global chicken population exceeds 26.5 billion, with more than 50 billion birds produced annually for consumption. Specialized breeds such as broilers and laying hens have been developed for meat and egg production, respectively. A hen bred for laying can produce over 300 eggs per year. Chickens are social animals with complex vocalizations and behaviors, and feature in folklore, religion, and literature across many societies. Their economic importance makes them a central component of global animal husbandry.

Did you know? The chicken is the closest living relative of the Tyrannosaurus rex among birds, based on protein analysis of preserved T. rex fossils.

Phylogeny

Chickens belong to the order Galliformes, which also includes turkeys, quails, and pheasants. Molecular studies show that the domestic chicken descended primarily from the red junglefowl, with possible minor contributions from the grey junglefowl (Gallus sonneratii), particularly influencing the yellow skin trait seen in many commercial breeds.

Domestication

Austronesia

Archaeological evidence suggests that chickens were first domesticated in Southeast Asia, spreading westward through trade routes and human migration. Austronesian seafarers are believed to have carried domesticated chickens to the Pacific islands thousands of years ago, predating European contact by millennia.

South Asia & China

Evidence of chicken domestication in the Indus Valley civilization dates back to around 2500 BCE. Ancient Chinese records also document chickens as early as 1400 BCE during the Shang dynasty.

Spread to Europe & Africa

Chickens arrived in Europe via the Middle East around 800 BCE and spread throughout the Roman Empire. They were introduced to Africa through Phoenician trade and later became a staple livestock across the continent.

The Role of Religion

In many ancient cultures, chickens held ritual significance. Cockfighting was practiced as a religious ceremony before it became a sport, and roosters appeared in ancient Persian and Greek religious symbolism.

Modern Industrial Farming

The 20th century saw the rise of mass poultry farming, transforming the chicken from a backyard animal into the most commercially farmed bird in the world.

Nutrition & Uses

Chicken is one of the primary sources of protein worldwide, low in fat relative to red meats. Eggs are considered a nutritionally complete food, containing essential amino acids, vitamins B12 and D, and healthy fats. Feathers are used in insulation and down products, while waste is often composted as a nitrogen-rich fertilizer.

Chemistry Note

Chickens require fresh water — H2O — at all times for healthy growth and optimal egg production. Water accounts for approximately 65% of a chicken's body weight.
